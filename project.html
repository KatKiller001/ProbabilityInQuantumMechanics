Probability in quantum mechanics

Dualism wave-particle

One of the first steps towards this theory was done in 1801, by Thomas Young.

In his experiment, Young placed a monochromatic light source that shines through two gaps, at a distance comparable to the wavelength of the light, onto a screen that registers the photon position.

If the light intensity is low, the photons appear to behave like particles, colliding with the screen at random spots.

If the light intensity is high, the photons that collide onto the screen respect the pattern of wave interference.

This experiment settled the centuries-long debate about the composition of light, stating its dualistic nature.

Quantization of energy

In the early 1900s, with the experiments on the photoelectric phenomenon, it was discovered that energy was not a continuous quantity, but it was quantized.

The experiment consisted in shining a monochromatic light onto a metallic surface.

The metallic surface released electrons inversely proportional to the light source wavelength, but the effect observed would not occur if the frequency didn't surpass a minimum value, even if the light was shined for prolonged periods.

This discovery showed that energy was not transferred in a continuous way, but it was transmitted in packets.

Schrodinger equation

De Broglie extended the dualism wave-particle to all corps, and correlated the mass of a corp and the wave function associated with it in an inversely proportional manner.

With this interpretation the wavelength of macro objects is negligible, but for elementary particles calculating the associated wave function becomes fundamental for studying them.

The equation that describes waves of matter was found by Schrodinger in 1926.

This equation, if calculated, gives us a complex quantity that describes the wave, but Schrodinger didn't explain what this wave was.

The physicist that explained what this wave was was Max Born, in particular he said that the waves of matter were waves of probability.

If we consider the square of Schrodinger's equation we obtain a real positive quantity that represents the probability of locating a particle in a given position.
